Licensing & Ownership

What happens if a license is not renewed

This is the single most consequential difference, and it is documented by the vendors themselves — verifiable, not opinion.

Current State — WatchGuard
7-day grace period after the security subscription expires, then antivirus, intrusion prevention, and content filtering are disabled and configuration changes can no longer be saved.
Source: WatchGuard documentation
Spectrum — Cisco Meraki
30-day grace period after a license expires, then the network devices shut down and become non-operational until new licensing is purchased.
Source: Cisco Meraki documentation
ButeraNet — UniFi
No per-device or per-feature licensing exists. There is nothing to expire. The hardware keeps running indefinitely, and the church owns it outright.
Source: UniFi product model
What ButeraNet offers instead: full ownership of the hardware, no required network-device licensing, and no recurring fee owed if Kings Cathedral ever chooses to move on — the equipment, the documentation, and the credentials remain the church’s, and everything keeps working. The relationship is held by service, not by a license that can be switched off.
Self-Sufficiency by Design

You are never locked in — not even to ButeraNet

The largest risk in the current setup is that IT knowledge lives with the provider, not with the church. If that provider leaves, the organization is stranded. Every ButeraNet engagement is built to remove that risk: the environment is documented and handed to Kings Cathedral as it is built.

Credentials
Every login — firewall, switches, server, Microsoft 365, ISP account — documented and handed to the church, stored securely.
Procedures
Written SOPs for routine tasks, so staff can handle day-to-day work without calling anyone.
Documentation
Network diagrams, device inventory, and a disaster-recovery plan — the full map of the environment, owned by Kings Cathedral.
The test of a good IT partner is whether you could replace them without disruption. With ButeraNet you always could — the hardware, the credentials, and the documentation are the church’s. The relationship continues on the strength of the service, not on dependency.

A Point of Fact

Spectrum: the verbal claim and the published spec

Spectrum’s representative verbally described 1 Gbps symmetric service. Spectrum’s published Business Internet specifications list asymmetric plans — not symmetric. True symmetric service is provided through Spectrum Dedicated Fiber — a premium product at a substantially higher monthly cost — and that fiber infrastructure is not yet available at this location. This is stated so leadership can confirm it directly against spectrum.com before relying on a verbal figure — a point of fact, not a criticism.

Security

Quietly reviewed, calmly handled

We quietly reviewed the church’s email and public-facing security. Here’s the plain-English picture — no jargon, no alarm.

The issue
Today, a stranger could send an email that looks like it’s from King’s to your staff or donors — the church’s domain isn’t yet set to block it. It’s the most common way ministries get targeted: “pastor needs gift cards” and fake-giving scams.
What you gain by fixing
Your people and donors can trust that an email from the church really is from the church. The fix is quick (about two weeks), no-cost, and handed to you — documented and owned, like everything else.
The cost of waiting
Left open, one convincing fake to a generous donor can cost real money — and the trust that took years to build. The exposure sits on the church’s name and its giving.
The specific findings are kept off this public page on purpose. Ask Travis for the short, private walkthrough — the path to closed is already mapped, and most of it costs nothing.
